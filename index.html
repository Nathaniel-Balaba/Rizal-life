Republic Act 1425: The Rizal Law

Understanding the legislation that made Rizal's works a cornerstone of Philippine education

Historical Background

Republic Act No. 1425, commonly known as the Rizal Law, was enacted on June 12, 1956. Authored by Senator Claro M. Recto, this legislation mandates the teaching of José Rizal's life, works, and writings in all schools, colleges, and universities in the Philippines. The law was created to inspire patriotism and national identity among Filipino students by studying the life and works of the national hero.

"The purpose of this Act is to honor José Rizal, to rekindle the spirit of patriotism and love of country, especially among the youth, and to instill appreciation for the works, writings, and contribution of José Rizal in the struggle against foreign domination."

— Excerpt from Republic Act No. 1425

Key Provisions

1

Mandatory Course

All schools, colleges, and universities must include courses on the life, works, and writings of José Rizal, particularly his novels Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo.

2

Original Texts

The law requires that the original or unexpurgated editions of Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo be used as basic texts. Translations to English, Filipino, and other local dialects are permitted.

3

Graduation Requirement

The completion of courses on Rizal is required for graduation from any college or university.

4

Availability of Materials

The Board of National Education is tasked with ensuring the availability of adequate copies of Rizal's works for all educational institutions.

Controversies and Implementation

The Rizal Law faced significant opposition, particularly from religious sectors concerned about anti-clerical themes in Rizal's novels. Senator Recto had to compromise by including a provision allowing students with religious objections to be exempt from reading the novels, though they would still need to study Rizal's life and other writings.

Despite initial challenges, the law has been successfully implemented across the Philippine educational system. Today, courses on Rizal's life and works are standard components of college curricula, typically as a 3-unit course covering his biography, political and social ideas, and literary contributions.

Significance and Impact

The Rizal Law has played a crucial role in preserving and promoting Rizal's legacy among generations of Filipinos. By making his works mandatory reading, the law ensures that his ideas on freedom, education, and national identity continue to influence Philippine society.

Moreover, the law has contributed to the development of Filipino nationalism and critical thinking among students, encouraging them to reflect on issues of social justice, governance, and cultural identity that remain relevant in contemporary Philippine society.

19th Century Philippines as Rizal's Context

Understanding the social, political, and cultural landscape that shaped Rizal's worldview

Spanish Colonial Rule

By the 19th century, the Philippines had been under Spanish colonial rule for over three centuries. The colonial administration was characterized by:

  • A centralized government headed by a Governor-General appointed by Spain
  • The encomienda system, which granted Spanish officials the right to collect tribute from the natives
  • The implementation of polo y servicio (forced labor)
  • Strong influence of the Catholic Church in governance and daily life

The Spanish colonial period saw the transformation of pre-colonial barangays into pueblos (towns) centered around a church and plaza, establishing the foundation for modern Philippine municipalities.

Manila under Spanish colonial rule in the 19th century

Social Structure and Classes

The social hierarchy in 19th century Philippines was rigid and based primarily on racial ancestry and wealth:

Peninsulares

Spanish-born Spaniards who occupied the highest government positions and enjoyed the most privileges

Insulares (Creoles)

Spaniards born in the Philippines who held significant wealth but faced discrimination from peninsulares

Mestizos

People of mixed ancestry (Spanish-Filipino or Chinese-Filipino) who formed the emerging middle class

Indios

Native Filipinos who constituted the majority of the population and occupied the lowest social positions

Rizal's family belonged to the principalia class, a privileged group of native elites who served as intermediaries between the Spanish authorities and the common people. This position afforded Rizal educational opportunities unavailable to most Filipinos, while also exposing him to the injustices of colonial rule.

Economic Conditions

The 19th century saw significant economic changes in the Philippines:

Opening of Philippine Ports (1834)

The opening of Philippine ports to international trade ended the galleon trade monopoly and integrated the Philippines into the global economy. This led to the rise of cash crop agriculture, particularly sugar, abaca, and tobacco.

The economic changes created a new class of wealthy Filipino landowners and merchants, including Rizal's family, who were involved in sugar production and leasing land from the Dominican friars. However, most Filipinos remained impoverished farmers or laborers working under exploitative conditions.

Religious and Educational Systems

Education under the Spanish colonial system

Religious Dominance

The Catholic Church wielded enormous influence in the Philippines, controlling education, owning vast tracts of land, and heavily influencing government policies. Religious orders (Dominicans, Augustinians, Franciscans, Recollects, and Jesuits) dominated various aspects of Filipino life.

Limited Education

Education was primarily religious in nature and limited to the elite. The University of Santo Tomas (founded in 1611) and Ateneo Municipal (now Ateneo de Manila) were among the few institutions of higher learning. Most Filipinos received minimal or no formal education, contributing to high illiteracy rates.

Reform and Revolutionary Movements

The 19th century witnessed the emergence of movements challenging Spanish colonial rule:

1

Secularization Movement

Filipino priests advocated for equal treatment with Spanish priests and greater roles in parish administration. The execution of three Filipino priests—Gomez, Burgos, and Zamora (GOMBURZA)—in 1872 for alleged involvement in the Cavite Mutiny became a turning point in Philippine history and deeply influenced Rizal.

2

Propaganda Movement

Educated Filipinos in Europe, including Rizal, Marcelo H. del Pilar, and Graciano Lopez Jaena, advocated for reforms through writings and political activities. They published La Solidaridad, a newspaper that called for Philippine representation in the Spanish Cortes, equal rights for Filipinos, and other reforms.

3

Revolutionary Movement

As peaceful reform efforts failed, more radical movements emerged. The Katipunan, founded by Andres Bonifacio in 1892, sought independence through armed revolution. The Philippine Revolution began in 1896, shortly after Rizal's execution.

Cultural and Intellectual Developments

The 19th century was a period of cultural awakening for the Philippines:

  • The emergence of a Filipino national consciousness and identity
  • Development of Filipino literature, including poetry, essays, and novels
  • Increased interest in pre-colonial Philippine history and culture
  • Influence of European Enlightenment ideas on educated Filipinos

This cultural renaissance, often referred to as the Filipino Enlightenment or Ilustrado movement, provided the intellectual foundation for both the reform and revolutionary movements. Rizal was at the forefront of this cultural awakening, using his literary works to articulate Filipino grievances and aspirations.

Rizal's Life: Family, Childhood, and Early Education

Exploring the formative years that shaped the national hero of the Philippines

Family Background

José Rizal was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, to a prosperous family of mixed ancestry:

Parents

  • Francisco Mercado Rizal: A Filipino farmer who leased land from the Dominican friars and was educated at the College of San José in Manila
  • Teodora Alonso Realonda: A highly educated woman from a prominent family in Manila who significantly influenced Rizal's intellectual development

The Rizal Family

Rizal's immediate family had a profound influence on his development and values

Rizal's Siblings - The Mercado-Rizal Children

José Rizal was the seventh of eleven children in the Mercado-Rizal family. Each of his siblings played important roles in his life and contributed to his development as a person and national hero:

1. Saturnina Rizal (1850-1913)

The eldest child and only sister older than José. She was known for her strong character and intelligence. Saturnina supported José's education financially and emotionally throughout his studies. She married Manuel Timoteo Hidalgo and was often called "Neneng" by the family.

2. Paciano Rizal (1851-1930)

José's only brother and closest confidant. Paciano was highly educated and served as José's mentor and protector. He sacrificed his own career to support José's education abroad. Paciano was also involved in revolutionary activities and was a member of the reform movement.

3. Narcisa Rizal (1852-1939)

Known as "Sisa" in the family, she was gentle and caring. Narcisa married Antonio López and was known for her devotion to her family. She lived the longest among all the siblings and witnessed the recognition of José as a national hero.

4. Olympia Rizal (1855-1887)

A cheerful and vivacious sister who married Silvestre Ubaldo. Olympia was known for her wit and humor, often bringing joy to family gatherings. She died young at the age of 32, which deeply saddened José during his time in Europe.

5. Lucia Rizal (1857-1919)

A quiet and reserved sister who married Mariano Herbosa, a town mate from Calamba. Lucia was known for her domestic skills and her dedication to her family. She maintained correspondence with José during his travels abroad.

6. María Rizal (1859-1945)

Known as "Biang," she married Daniel Faustino Cruz. María was particularly close to José and was known for her intelligence and strong personality. She was one of the siblings who actively supported José's endeavors and shared his patriotic sentiments.

8. Concepción Rizal (1862-1865)

Known as "Concha," she was José's younger sister who died at the tender age of three. Her death deeply affected young José and was one of his earliest encounters with loss and mortality. This experience influenced his later reflections on life and death.

9. Josefa Rizal (1865-1945)

Known as "Panggoy," she remained unmarried throughout her life and was considered the most religious among the siblings. Josefa was epileptic but was known for her gentle nature and devotion to the family. She took care of their parents in their old age.

10. Trinidad Rizal (1868-1951)

Known as "Trining," she also remained unmarried and was very close to José. Trinidad was entrusted with José's last poem "Mi Último Adiós" (My Last Farewell) hidden in an alcohol stove before his execution. She was instrumental in preserving José's memory and legacy.

11. Soledad Rizal (1870-1929)

The youngest of the Rizal children, known as "Choleng." She married Pantaleon Quintero and was known for her beauty and grace. Despite being the youngest, Soledad was mature beyond her years and maintained the family's unity after José's death.

Family Influence on José

The Rizal siblings created a nurturing environment that fostered José's intellectual and moral development. Their collective support, particularly from Paciano and Saturnina, enabled José to pursue his education and eventually become the national hero. The family's values of education, patriotism, and service to others were instilled in all the children and were particularly evident in José's life and works.

Social Status

The Rizal family belonged to the principalia class, a privileged group of native elites who served as intermediaries between Spanish authorities and common Filipinos. They were relatively wealthy landowners and merchants, which afforded Rizal educational opportunities unavailable to most Filipinos of his time. However, despite their status, the family still experienced discrimination under the Spanish colonial system.

Childhood and Early Influences

1

Early Childhood (1861-1867)

Rizal grew up in a comfortable home in Calamba, surrounded by books, art, and music. From an early age, he displayed remarkable intelligence and curiosity. His mother, Teodora, taught him to read and write before he was five years old and instilled in him a love for literature and learning.

2

Formative Experiences

Several childhood experiences profoundly influenced Rizal's worldview:

  • The story of the moth and the flame, which his mother told him, teaching him about sacrifice and persistence
  • Witnessing the unjust arrest of his mother on false charges of attempting to poison her sister-in-law
  • Learning about the execution of the three Filipino priests (GOMBURZA) in 1872, which awakened his sense of injustice
3

Artistic and Literary Beginnings

Even as a child, Rizal showed remarkable artistic talent. He created clay sculptures, drew sketches, and wrote poetry. His uncle, José Alberto, encouraged his artistic pursuits and taught him painting, sketching, and sculpture. These early creative expressions foreshadowed his later literary and artistic achievements.

Young Rizal studying in his childhood home in Calamba

Early Education

Home Education

Rizal's first teachers were his mother and private tutors. Teodora Alonso taught him reading and writing, while private tutors instructed him in Latin and Spanish. This home education provided him with a strong foundation in languages and literature.

Formal Schooling

Rizal's formal education began at age nine when he was sent to Biñan, Laguna, to study under Justiniano Aquino Cruz. Though he initially struggled with homesickness, he quickly excelled academically, surpassing many older students. His experiences in Biñan, including encounters with bullying and unfair treatment, further shaped his character and determination.

Academic Journey

Ateneo Municipal (1872-1877)

At age 11, Rizal enrolled at the Ateneo Municipal in Manila, a Jesuit institution that offered quality education. Despite initial challenges:

  • He excelled academically, consistently earning top marks
  • He won literary contests and medals in various subjects
  • He developed his artistic talents under the guidance of Spanish professors
  • He graduated with the highest honors (sobresaliente) in 1877

University of Santo Tomas (1877-1882)

After Ateneo, Rizal enrolled at the University of Santo Tomas to study medicine, specializing in ophthalmology to help cure his mother's developing eye condition. His experiences at UST were less positive than at Ateneo:

  • He encountered discrimination from Dominican professors
  • He witnessed the gap in educational methods between Spanish and progressive European universities
  • He became increasingly aware of social injustices in the colonial system
  • Despite challenges, he continued to excel in his studies

Literary and Intellectual Development

During his early education, Rizal developed the intellectual foundations that would later define his contributions to Philippine society:

Multilingualism

By his teenage years, Rizal was proficient in multiple languages, including Tagalog, Spanish, Latin, and Greek. He would later learn many more languages during his travels abroad.

Literary Works

He began writing poetry and essays at a young age. His early poem "Sa Aking Mga Kabata" ("To My Fellow Children") is believed to have been written when he was just eight years old.

Critical Thinking

His education fostered a critical mindset that allowed him to question the prevailing social and political conditions in the Philippines.

"Ang hindi magmahal sa sariling wika, masahol pa sa hayop at malansang isda."

("One who does not love his own language is worse than an animal and a stinking fish.") — Attributed to young Rizal in "Sa Aking Mga Kabata"

Rizal's early life and education laid the groundwork for his later achievements as a writer, scholar, and national hero. The values instilled by his family, his educational experiences, and his early exposure to injustice shaped his character and prepared him for his future role in advocating for reforms in the Philippines.

Rizal's Life: Higher Education and Life Abroad

Exploring Rizal's intellectual journey through Europe and his development as a scholar and reformist

Decision to Study Abroad

In 1882, at the age of 21, Rizal made the momentous decision to continue his studies in Europe. Several factors influenced this decision:

Academic Aspirations

Rizal sought to complete his medical education, particularly in ophthalmology, to help treat his mother's eye condition. He was also drawn to the advanced educational systems of European universities.

Discrimination at UST

He experienced discrimination at the University of Santo Tomas, where Filipino students were often treated as inferior to their Spanish counterparts.

Political Awakening

The execution of GOMBURZA in 1872 and other injustices had awakened his political consciousness. He believed that education in Europe would provide him with knowledge and perspectives to advocate for reforms.

Family Support

His brother Paciano and other family members supported his decision, recognizing his potential and the value of European education for his development.

European Education

Spain (1882-1885)

Rizal's first destination was Spain, where he enrolled at the Universidad Central de Madrid:

  • He pursued a licentiate in medicine
  • He simultaneously studied philosophy and letters
  • He completed his medical degree in 1884
  • He earned his licentiate in philosophy and letters in 1885 with excellent grades

France and Germany (1885-1887)

After Spain, Rizal continued his medical specialization:

  • In Paris, he worked as an assistant to Dr. Louis de Wecker, a renowned ophthalmologist
  • In Heidelberg, Germany, he studied under Dr. Otto Becker, completing his ophthalmology specialization
  • He also attended lectures in sciences, languages, and other subjects at various German universities

Rizal studying at a European university, where he expanded his knowledge and worldview

Intellectual and Cultural Exposure

Rizal's time in Europe exposed him to a wealth of intellectual and cultural influences:

1

Enlightenment Ideas

He immersed himself in Enlightenment philosophy, studying works by Voltaire, Rousseau, and other thinkers who advocated for reason, liberty, and equality. These ideas reinforced his belief in the importance of education and human rights.

2

Scientific Advancement

He witnessed Europe's scientific and technological progress, which contrasted sharply with the Philippines' underdevelopment under Spanish rule. This strengthened his conviction that the Philippines needed modernization and educational reforms.

3

Liberal Politics

He observed democratic movements and constitutional governments in action, providing him with models for the political reforms he would advocate for the Philippines.

4

Cultural Diversity

He traveled extensively throughout Europe, experiencing different cultures, languages, and social systems. This broadened his perspective and helped him envision a more progressive future for the Philippines.

The Propaganda Movement

While in Europe, Rizal became actively involved in the Propaganda Movement, a group of Filipino expatriates advocating for reforms in the Spanish colonial administration of the Philippines:

La Solidaridad

Rizal contributed articles to La Solidaridad, the movement's newspaper founded in 1889. Through this platform, he and fellow reformists advocated for:

  • Philippine representation in the Spanish Cortes (parliament)
  • Equal rights for Filipinos and Spaniards
  • Secularization of Philippine parishes
  • Freedom of speech, assembly, and press
  • Educational reforms in the Philippines

Key figures in the Propaganda Movement who worked alongside Rizal included:

Marcelo H. del Pilar

Journalist and editor of La Solidaridad

Graciano López Jaena

Orator and founder of La Solidaridad

Mariano Ponce

Writer and one of the movement's key organizers

Literary Works and Intellectual Contributions

Rizal's novels Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo became powerful critiques of Spanish colonial rule

During his time in Europe, Rizal produced his most significant literary works:

Noli Me Tangere (1887)

Written in Spanish and published in Berlin, this novel exposed the abuses of Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines, particularly by the friars. The title, meaning "Touch Me Not," refers to Christ's words to Mary Magdalene after his resurrection and symbolizes the untouchable nature of the colonial system's problems.

El Filibusterismo (1891)

The sequel to Noli Me Tangere, published in Ghent, Belgium, this darker novel depicts the failure of reform efforts and explores themes of revenge, revolution, and redemption. The title refers to "subversion" or "sedition," reflecting the more radical tone of the work.

Beyond his novels, Rizal made other significant intellectual contributions:

Annotations to Morga's Sucesos

Rizal annotated Antonio de Morga's historical work "Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas" (Events in the Philippine Islands), published in 1609. Through his annotations, Rizal challenged the Spanish narrative of Philippine history and highlighted the advanced civilization that existed in the Philippines before colonization.

Scientific Research

He conducted ethnographic, anthropological, and linguistic research, contributing to the scientific understanding of Southeast Asian cultures. He discovered several new species of animals, including a type of flying dragon, which was named after him (Draco rizali).

Essays and Articles

He wrote numerous essays on Philippine society, culture, and politics, including "The Indolence of the Filipinos" and "The Philippines A Century Hence," which analyzed the causes of social problems and predicted the future of the country.

Return to the Philippines and Exile

After years abroad, Rizal returned to the Philippines in 1887, motivated by:

  • Concern for his family, who were facing harassment from Spanish authorities and conflicts with Dominican friars over land
  • Desire to practice ophthalmology and treat his mother's eye condition
  • Wish to experience firsthand the conditions in his homeland

However, his return was short-lived. The publication of Noli Me Tangere had made him a marked man. After only six months, he was forced to leave due to threats to his life and growing tensions with Spanish authorities.

Rizal's second return to the Philippines in 1892 led to his exile to Dapitan in Mindanao, where he spent four productive years (1892-1896):

Medical Practice

He established a medical practice, treating locals and even performing a successful cataract operation on his mother

Educational Initiatives

He founded a school for boys, teaching languages, sciences, and physical education

Community Development

He implemented agricultural and economic projects, including a water system for the community

Scientific Research

He continued his scientific studies, collecting specimens and corresponding with European scientists

Legacy of Rizal's Education and Life Abroad

Rizal's European education and experiences profoundly shaped his worldview and contributions to Philippine society:

1

Intellectual Foundation

His education provided him with the knowledge and analytical skills to articulate the grievances and aspirations of Filipinos effectively.

2

Reform Vision

His exposure to liberal ideas and progressive societies in Europe informed his vision for reforms in the Philippines, emphasizing education, equality, and justice.

3

National Consciousness

His writings, particularly his novels, awakened a sense of national identity among Filipinos and exposed the injustices of colonial rule to an international audience.

4

Educational Model

His own pursuit of knowledge and excellence set a standard for Filipino students and intellectuals, emphasizing the value of education in personal and national development.
